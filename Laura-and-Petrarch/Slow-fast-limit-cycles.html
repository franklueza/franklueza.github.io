If ε is small, the variable Z(t) is slow with respect to L(t) and P(t), so that the singular perturbation method [6], [7] can be used. Roughly speaking, the method affirms that the system can be decomposed into fast and slow components (in the present case the fast subsystem is described by (6), (7) with Z - constant, and the slow one by (15)). Such a decomposition allows one to construct the so-called singular orbits, which are composed of concatenations of alternate fast and slow transitions. The main result of singular perturbation theory is that, under suitable regularity assumptions (which are satisfied in the present case) any orbit of the system approaches, for ε → 0 , the corresponding singular orbit. This result has been proved first by Tikhonov [8] for the simple case of singular orbits not passing through bifurcation points of the fast subsystem and by Pontryagin [9] for the case of singular orbits passing through nondegenerate (i.e., quadratic) fold bifurcations of the fast subsystem. Remarks on the use of this method for detecting limit cycles in second- and third-order systems can be found in Muratori and Rinaldi [10], [11], while examples of applications can be found in [12], [13], [1
